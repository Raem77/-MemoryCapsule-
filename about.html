About this project

What makes certain fragments worth keeping? A photograph, a note, a drawing, or a small object can outlast the moment it belongs to, carrying traces of memory through its physical presence. Gathered over time, these materials form a personal archive shaped by repetition, attachment, and the quiet persistence of nostalgia. Memory is approached not as a complete record, but as something partial returning through details, textures, and familiar images that hold emotional weight beyond their original context. Early memories often remain incomplete, filtered through the lens of innocence, imagination, and emotion. Encounter preserved fragments: a digital capsule where the past is held through objects that continue to resist being forgotten. This project began with a real box that I had been adding to for years, without ever really deciding to. It slowly turned into a time capsule, filled with small things I couldn’t bring myself to throw away, even if I couldn’t fully explain why. Each object held onto something a feeling, a moment, a version of myself that I didn’t want to lose. Translating that into a digital space became a way for me to open it up, to move beyond the physical limits of a container and let it expand into something I could navigate and return to differently. The visuals of the site come from the kinds of drawings I used to make as a child the ones that feel instantly familiar when you come across them years later. Taking inspiration from the hand-drawn world of Le Petit Prince, I leaned into a doodle-like style that carries a sense of softness and a little whimsy. At its core, this project stays rooted in the relationship between memory and physicality, the idea that the things I’ve chosen to keep, and the way I’ve kept them, reveal something about who I am and who I’ve been.

*Tiny Planet

As we move through different chapters of life, the spaces we inhabit begin to reflect them. Objects accumulate a magnet from a country visited, a ticket stub in a drawer, a postcard pinned to a wall each one a quiet remnant of a person, a place, or a moment that has since passed. The home becomes its own kind of archive, shaped not by intention but by the slow gathering of lived experience. The ‘Tiny Planet’ extends this idea outward: a small world built from the residue of memory, where the familiar and the distant coexist in the same orbit, held together by the same pull that keeps us reaching back.
